Chapter 8: Decision Making

The decision making process

Preface

  • Managers at all levels make decisions

  • However, making decisions aren’t just limited to managers, all organizational members make decisions that affect their jobs and the organization they work for.

8 steps of the decision-making process
Steps of the decision-making processed, example by a manager deciding to buy a laptop computer

1. Identifying a problem

  • Every decision starts with a problem, a discrepancy between an existing and a desired condition

  • Managers also have to be cautious not to confuse problems with symptoms of a problem

2. Identify Decision Criteria

  • Once one identified a problem, they must identify the criteria important or relevant to the problem.

  • Most decision makers have criteria guiding their decision even if not explicitly stated.

3. Allocate weights to Criteria

  • If the relevant criteria aren’t equally important, the decision maker must weight the items in order to give them the correct priority in the decision.

4. Develop Alternatives

  • Decision makers list the viable alternatives to solve the problem

  • The alternatives are only listed—not evaluated

5. Analyse Alternatives

  • After listing data, the decision maker must evaluate them.

  • When you multiply each alternative by alternative weight, you get a weighted alternative

6. Select an alternative

  • Choosing either the best alternative or the one with the highest total in step 5

  • Note that the alternative that’s best according to the numbers may not be the best alternative due to having incorrect weights.\

  • Therefore, if one find that the alternative is “not right”, the decision maker need to decide if a review of criteria is needed before implementing the alternative

7. Implement the alternative

  • Conveying the decision to those affected by the decision and getting their commitment to it

8. Evaluate Decision Effectiveness

  • Evaluating the outcome and/or result of the experiment to see if the problem is resolved. If the problem still exists, then managers need to see what’s wrong:

  • Some suggested questions (from the textbook):

    • Was the problem incorrectly defined?
    • Were errors made when evaluating alternatives?
    • Was the right alternative selected but poorly implemented?

Chapter 9: Planning

Purposes and Types of plans

Definition

  • Planning involves defining goals, establishing an overall strategy to reach those goals, and developing a comprehensive set of plans to integrate and coordinate the work needed to achieve the goals

Purpose

  1. Provides direction: letting employees and people know what their organization is trying to achieve and contribute to such goals.
  2. Reduces uncertainty: forcing managers to look ahead, anticipate and consider the impact of change, as well as develop responses. Planning won’t eliminate uncertainty, but managers plan to respond effectively.
  3. Minimizes waste and uncertainty: Work coordinates around plan, therefore reducing inefficiencies and mistakes can be corrected.
  4. Establishes goals or standard uses in controlling: When control, managers see when the plan has been carried out. Without plans, there would be no measure of work effort

Types of plans

By scale

  • Strategic plans are plans that apply to the whole organization and establish its goals.

  • Operational plans are plans that encompasses a particular operational area of the organization.

By length

  • Long-term plans are plans with a timeframe of more than 3 years.

  • Short-term plans are plans covering 1 year or less.

  • Intermediate plans are plans that cover lengths in-between

  • Note that these classifications are common, but managers are free to use whatever timeframe they want.

By flexibility

  • Specific plans are plans that are clearly defined and leave no room for errors/interpretation, eliminates ambiguity and problems understanding when stated.

  • Directional plans are flexible plans that set out general guidelines. They provide focus but don’t lock managers into specific actions.

By usage

  • A single-use plan is a one-time plan specifically designed to meet the needs of an unique situation.

  • Standing plans are ongoing plans that provide guidance for activities done repeatedly

Chapter 10: Managing strategically

The strategic management process

Definitions

  • Strategic management is what managers do to develop the strategies in an organization.

  • Strategies are the decisions and actions that determine the long-run performance of the organization, e.g how to compete successfully, or how to attract and satisfy customers.

The Strategic Management Process

1. Identify the Current Missions, Goals and Strategies

  • Every organization needs a mission: a statement of its purpose.
  • Managers need to identify what it’s in business to do, however the mission statement sometimes too limiting.
Typical components included in a mission statement

2. Conduct an External Analysis

  • Managers need to know, e.g. what their competition is doing, what legislations might affect the organization, etc.

  • Managers need to examine both the specific and general environment to see what trends and changes is occurring.

  • After examination, managers need to assess what they have learned in terms of opportunities the organization can use and threats that it must face.

    • Oppoturnities are positive trends in external environments.

    • Threats are negative trends.

3. Conduct an internal analysis.

Definitions

  • An organization’s resources are its assets: financial, physical, human, intangible, that are used to develop, manufacture, or deliver its products or services to customers.

  • An organization’s capabilities are its skills and abilities that enable it to do the work it does in its business.

  • An organization’s core competencies is its major value-creating skills, capabilities and resources that determine its competitive advantage.

After conducting an internal analysis

  • After conducting an internal analysis, managers should be able to identify organizational strengths and weaknesses.

    • Strengths of an organization is the activities that it does well or the unique resources it has.

    • Weaknesses of an organization is the activities the organization does not do well or resources it needs but currently doesn’t posess.

  • The combination of external and internal analysis is also called **SWOT Analysis (Strengths, Weaknesses, Oppoturnities, Threats)

4. Formulate Strategies

  • Once the SWOT Analysis is complete, managers need to develop and evaluate strategic alternatives and select strategies that capitalise on the strengths, exploit oppoturnities, correct the organization’s weaknesses and buff it against threats

5. Implement Strategies

  • After strategies are formed, they must be implemented

  • Different things organizations can do to implement a new strategy:
Ways to implement a strategy
6. Evaluate results

  • How affective have the strategies been?

  • What adjustment, if any, were necessary?

Growth Strategy

  • Growth Strategy is the strategy that’s used when an organization wants to expand the number markets served or number of products offered, either through new or current businesses.

Organizations grow via either concentration, vertical integration, horizontal integration or diversification.

  • Growth through concentration is achieved when an organization concentrates on its primary line of business and increases the number of products offered or markets served in this primary business.

    • No other firms are acquired or merged, the organization increase business by increasing its operations.

  • Backward vertical integration is when an organization becomes its own supplier to control its inputs.

  • Forward vertical integration is when an organization becomes its own distributor and able to control its output.

  • Horizontal Integration is when a company grows by combining with others in the same industry

  • Related diversification is when a company grows by merging or acquiring firms in different but related industries.

  • Unrelated diversification is when a company combines with firms in different and unrelated industries.

Stability Strategy

A stability strategy
is characterized by a lack of significant change in what the organization is doing.
  • There are times when the industry is rapidly changing, when the managers might decide the course of action is to sit tight and see what happens.
  • Owners of small businesses, may feel that the business is successful enough as-is, and that already fulfilled their goals nor do they want hassles of growing businesses.

Renewal Strategy

  • A Renewal Strategy is a strategy that addresses declining performance

There are 2 main types of renewal strategies:

  1. A. retrenchment strategy is a short-run renewal strategy used for minor performance problems
  2. A turnaround strategy is a strategy used when problems are more serious.

Managers do 2 things for both strategies: cutting costs and restrucring organizational operations. However, turnaround strategies involves more extensive use of those strategies rather than a retrenchment strategy

Competitive stratgies (Porter)

Preface

In any industry, managers face 5 threats:

  • Threat of new entrants. Factors such as economies of scale, brand loyalty, and capital requirements determine how easy or hard it is for new competitors to enter an industry.

  • Threat of substitutes. Factors such as switching costs and buyer loyalty determine the degree to which customers are likely to buy a substitute product.

  • Bargaining power of buyers. Factors such as number of customers in the market, customer information, and the availability of substitutes determine the amount of influence that buyers have in an industry.

  • Bargaining power of suppliers. Factors such as the degree of supplier concentration and availability of substitute inputs determine the amount of power that suppliers have over firms in the industry.

  • Current rivalry. Factors such as industry growth rate, increasing or falling demand, and product differences determine how intense the competitive rivalry will be among firms currently in the industry.

Choosing a competitive strategy

Porter suggests that strategies can be used to give the organization a competitive advantage.

  • A cost leadership strategy is a strategy in which the organization sets to become the lowest cost producer.

    • Overhead is kept to a minimum, and the firm does everything it can to cut costs.

  • A differentiation strategy is a strategy in which a company seeks to offer unique products that are widely valued by customers.

    • The key to this strategy is that the product or service chosen must set the firm apart, and be significant enough to justify a price premium that exceeds the cost of differentiating.

  • A focus strategy is a strategy in which a business pursues a cost or differentation advantage in a narrow industry segment.

    • Segments can be based on product variety, customer type, distribution channel, or geographical location.

The six key elements of organizational design.

Work specialization

  • Work specialization is the degree to which activities in an organization are subdivided into tasks, also known as division of labor, making efficient use of the diversity of skills workers have.

  • Early proponents of work specialization believed it could increase to productivity. However, at some point, the human diseconomies from it exceed the economic advantages.
Economies and diseconomies of work specialization

Today’s view

  • Managers today see work specialization as an important organizing mechanism, NOT as a source of ever-increasing productivity. They recognize its benefits and problems

  • However, it still has its place and some organization.

Departmentalization

  • Departmentalization is the basis of which jobs are grouped together.

  • There are five common forms of departmentalization, however organizations may develop their own unique classification.
The five common forms of Departmentalization

Today’s view

  • Most large organizations continue to use combinations of most or all types of departmentalization.

  • One popular trend of departmentalization is the increasing use of customer departmentalization, since they are important to a business. Another trend is the use of teams, one specific type of which is a cross-functional team, a team of individuals from various functional specialities.

Chain of Command.

  • The chain of command is a continuous line of authority that extends from upper levels of organization to the lower levels and clarifies who reports to whom.

  • The chain of command needs three important concepts: authority, responsibility and unity of command

Authority

  • Authority refers to the rights inherent in a mangerial position. Managers had authority to coordinate and oversee the work of others.

  • Early writers assumed that rights and power are the sole source of influence and that if an order were given, it would be obeyed. They distinguished two forms of authority: line authority and staff authority:

    • Line theory entitles a manager to direct the work of an employee
    • Staff theory is the authority that have been created to support, assist and advise those holding line authority.

  • Chester Barnard proposed the accpetance theory of authority, stating that authority does not come from the manager but from the willingness to accept it by subordinates.

  • He contended that subordinates will accept orders only if the following conditions are satisfied:

    • They understand the order
    • They feel the order is consistent with the purpose of the organization
    • The order does NOT conflict with their personal beliefs
    • They are able to perform the task as directed .

Responsibility

  • Responsibility is the obligation or expectation to perform assigned duties.

  • Assigning work authority without responsibility and accountability can create opportunities for abuse, and none should be accountable for things that they had no authority for.

Unity of command

  • The unity of command states that one person ONLY reports to one manager. Conflicting demands from bosses when otherwise can cuase problems

Today’s view

  • While the elements were believed to be essential, they are far less important today, such as by the use of Information Technology, allowing employees to access information only available to managers, or communicate without the chain of commands

  • They may find themselves reporting to more than one boss, violating the unity of command principle, but it can work if communication, conflict, etc are well-managed by involved parties.

Span of control

  • The span of control is defined as the ==number of employees == a manager can effectively and efficiently manage

  • The traditional view: managers couldn’t and shouldn’t manage more than 5-6 employees.

  • The wider the span, the more successful the organization if all things being equal.

  • However, when the span becomes too large, employee performance will suffer because managers may no longer have the time to provide the necessary leadership and support.

Today’s view

  • Many factors influence the number of employees a manager can efficiently and effectively manage, including but not limited to:

    • The skills and abilities of the manager
    • The characteristics of the work being done

  • The trend in recent years has been towards larger spans of control, with is consistent with managers’ efforts to speed up decision making, increase flexibility, get closer to customers, empower employees, and reduce costs. They also recognize that they can handle a wider span when employees know their jobs well and when they understand organizational processes.

Centralization and Decentralization

  • Centralization is the degree to which decision making takes place at the upper level of the organization,

  • Decentralization is the reverse, the degree of which decision is made by lower-level employees.

  • Early writers prposed that the degree of centralization depended on the situation, but today organizations were much more complex and responsive to dynamic changes in their problems.
Factors that affect an organization’s degree of centralization

Today’s View

  • Managers today choose the amount of centralization that allows them to best implement their decisions and achieve organizational goals.

  • As organizations become more flexible and responsibe, a trend known as employee enpowerment decentralizing decision making, giving employees more authority for decisions. In larger companies, lower level managers are “closer to the action” and have more detailed knowledge about problems and how best to solve them compared to higher level managers.

Formalization

  • Formalization refers to how standardized an organization’s jobs are and the extent exployee behavior is guided by rules and procedures

Today’s View

  • While **some ** formalization is important for consistency and control. today’s organizations seem to be less reliant on struct rules and standardization to guide and regulate employees behavior.
  • Many organizations allowed employees some freedom to make some decisions.

  • After developing a pool of candidates, the next step is to determine who would be the most fit for the job, in a process called the selection process

What is selection?

  • Selection involves predicting which applicants will be successful when hired. A decision can result in four different outcomes, two correct and two erroneous.
The possible decision outcomes of selection

  • A decision is considered correct when the applicant was predicted to be successful and proved to be successful on the job (successfully accepted), OR, they were predicted to be unsuccessful and was indeed so when hired (successfully rejected).

  • A reject error occurs when candidates that would have performed successfully were rejected, and a accept error occurs when accepting those who perform poorly.

  • Reject errors and accept errors both have significant consequences to the organization, and therefore the thrust of selection activity is to reduce both, while increasing the probability of making correct ones. To do so, managers use selection procedures that are valid and reliable.

Validity and Reliability

  • Validity of a selection device is a proven relationship between the selection device and the job criteriation. Managers have a burden to show that selection devices they use to differ between applicants have to be related to job performance

  • A selection device also needs to showcase reliability, if it measures the same thing consistently, over time assuming others don’t change.

  • A growing number of companies is using “quality of fill”, a device that concerns the five key factors: employee retention, performance evaluations, number of first-year hires who make it into high-potential training programs, number of employees who are promoted, and what surveys of new hires indicate.

The four main leader behavioural theories

University of Iowa studies

  • The UoI studies explored three leadership styles to find which was the most effective:

    • The autocratic style described a leader who dictated work methods, make unilateral decisions, and limit employee participation.
    • The democratic style described a leader that involves everyone in decision making, delegated authority , and use feedback as an oppoturnity for coaching.
    • The laissez-faire style described a leader who let their group make decisions and complete the work in whatever way it saw fit

  • Studies of the autocratic and democratic styles showed mixed results, but group members were more satisfied under a democratic leader than under an autocratic one.

  • The dilemma of higher performance vs member satisfaction, is a nature that’s also recognized by other (behavioural) studies.

The Ohio State Studies

  • The OS studies identified two major dimensions of leader behaviour:

    • The first one is initiating structure, the extent to which a leader defined their roles and the roles of group members in attaining goals.
    • The second one is consideration, the extent to which a leader has work relationships characterized by mutual trust and respect for group members’ ideas and feelings.

  • A leader’s who high in both dimensions sometimes achieve high group task performance and member satisfaction, but not always.

University of Michigan Studies

  • The UoM studies also came up with two dimensions: employee oriented and production oriented.

  • Leaders who are employee oriented were described as emphasizing interpersonal relationships, while in contrast, leaders that are production oriented tended to emphasize the task aspects of the job.

  • Unlike the other studies, the UoM study concluded that leaders who are employee oriented have both high group productivity and high member satisfaction.

Contingency theories

The Fielder Model (FM)

  • The Fiedler Model proposed that ==effective group management depended on the proper match of the leader’s style and the amount of control and influence in the situation. It is based on the premise that a certain leadership style would be most effective in different situations

  • The key to this model would be to define the leadership styles and the situations and identify the appropiate combinations of style and situation.

  • Fiedler proposed that a key factor in leadership success was an individual’s basic leadership style, either task oriented or relationship oriented. To measure the style, he developed the least-preferred co-worker (LPC) questionnaire, consisting of 18 pairs of contrasting adjectives. If the leader described the least-preferred co-worker in positive terms (or high LPC score) then they are considered to have good relationship with others, and is therefore relationship oriented, and vice versa.

  • After the leadership style had been assessed through the LPC, the situation needs to be evaluated in order to match the leader. Fiedler’s research identified three contingency dimensions that defined the key situational factors:

    • Leader-member relations: The degree of trust, confidence, and respect employees have for their leader, rated as good or poor
    • Task structure: The degree to which job assignments were formalized and structured, rated as high or low
    • Position power: the degree of influence a leader had over activities such hiring, discipline, promotions, and salary increases, rated as strong or weak

  • Each situation was evaluated in these 3 variables, when combined produced 8 situations that are either favorable or unfavorable for the leader. Fiedler concluded that task-oriented leaders performed better in very favourable situations and in very unfavourable situations, while relationship-oriented leaders performed better in moderately favourable situations.
The Fiedler Model, and evaluation of performance based on situation favourableness
  • Since Fiedler’s model is fixed, that means in order to improve leader effectiveness, there are only two ways: Bring in a new leader whose style better fits the situation, or change the situation to fit the leader. This also leads to the criticism of the theory that a person can’t change, and situation variables are difficult to asses.

Situational Leadership Theory (SLT)

  • Developed by Paul Hersey and Ken Blanchard, the Situational Leadership Theory (SLT) is a contingency theory that focuses on the followers’ readiness. The theory reflects the idea that it’s the followers who accept or reject the leader, and effectiveness of the group depended on them, which is an important dimension that other theories overlooked or under-emphasized.

  • Hersey and Blanchard defined readiness as the extent to which people have the ability and willingness to accomplish a specific task.

  • SLT uses the same two leadership dimension as FM: task and relationship behaviors. However, it also considers each as high or low and combine into four distinct styles:

    • Telling (high task–low relationship): The leader defines roles and tells people what, how, when, and where to do various tasks.
    • Selling (high task–high relationship)—The leader provides both directive and supportive behaviour.
    • Participating (low task–high relationship)—The leader and followers share in decision making; the main role of the leader is facilitating and communicating.
    • Delegating (low task–low relationship)—The leader provides little direction or support.

  • The final component of SLT is the four stages of follower readiness:

    • R1—People are both unable and unwilling to take responsibility for doing something. Followers aren’t competent or confident.
    • R2—People are unable but willing to do the necessary job tasks. Followers are motivated but lack the appropriate skills.
    • R3—People are able but unwilling to do what the leader wants. Followers are competent but don’t want to do something.
    • R4—People are both able and willing to do what is asked of them.

  • As followers reach higher levels of readiness, the leader responds not only by decreasing control over their activities but also by decreasing relationship behaviours.
The Situational Leadership Theory
  • SLT acknowledges the importance of followers and builds on the logic that leaders can compensate for ability and motivational limitations in followers.

Path-goal theory (PGT)

  • Developed by Robert House, PGT is the current most respected approaches to leadership, stating that a leader’s job is to assist followers and provide direction or support needed to ensure their goals are compatible with the group or organization.

  • House identified four leadership behaviors:

    • Directive leader: The leader lets subordinates know what’s expected of them, schedules work to be done, and gives specific guidance on how to accomplish tasks.
    • Supportive leader: The leader shows concern for the needs of followers and is friendly.
    • Participative leader: —The leader consults with group members and uses their suggestions before making a decision.
    • Achievement-oriented leader: —The leader sets challenging goals and expects followers to perform at their highest level.

  • In contrast to Fiedler’s view that a leader can’t change his or her behaviour, House assumed that leaders are flexible and can display any or all of these leadership styles, depending on the situation.

  • The PGT proposes two situational or contingency variables that moderate the leadership behaviour-outcome relationship: the environment outside control of the follower, and those that are part of the personal characteristics of the follower.

  • Environmental factors determine the type of leader behaviour required if subordinate outcomes are to be maximized; personal characteristics of the follower determine how the environment and leader behaviour are interpreted.

Early Theories of Motivation

Maslow’s Hierarchy of Needs Theory

  • Maslow suggested that each person has a hierarchy of five needs:

    1. Physiological needs: A person’s need for food, drink, shelter, sexual satisfaction, and other physical requirements.
    2. Safety needs: The need for security and protection from physical and emotional harm, as well as assurance that physical needs will continue to be met.
    3. Social needs: The need for affection, belonging-ness, acceptance, and friendship.
    4. Esteem needs. Internal esteem factors such as self-respect, autonomy, and achievement, and external esteem factors such as status, recognition, and attention.
    5. Self-actualization needs. Growth, achieving one’s potential, and self-fulfillment; the drive to become what one is capable of becoming.

  • Maslow argued that each level in the hierarchy must be substantially satisfied before the next is activated, and once a need is substantially satisfied, it no longer motivates behavior. In other words, they move up the needs hierarchy
The needs hierarchy

  • While no need is fully satisfied, a substantially satisfied need will longer motivate an individual.

  • Therefore, motivating someone requires understanding the level the person is in and focus on satisfying needs at that level or higher, according to Maslow.

  • Despite the ease of understanding and intuitive logic, Maslow had no empirical support for his theory, and studies sought to validate it couldn’t.

McGregor’s Theory X and Theory Y

  • Douglas McGregor is best known for proposing two assumptions about human nature: Theory X and Theory Y:

    • Theory X assumes that employees dislike work, are lazy and avoid responsibility, and must be coerced to perform.
    • Theory Y assumes employees enjoy work, seek out and accept respondibility, and exercise self-direction.

  • Unfortunately, no evidence confirms that either set of assumptions is valid.

Herzberg’s Two-Factor Theory (also called motivation-hygiene theory)
Herzberg’s Two-Factor Theory, findings from his survey work

  • Frederick’s Herzberg’s two-factor theory proposes that intrinsic factors are related to job satisfaction, whereas extrinsic factors are associated with job dissatisfaction.

  • He belived that the opposite of satisfaction was NOT dissatisfaction, and removing dissastisfactory characteristics from a job would not necessarily make it more satifying. Instead, he proposed that his findings indicated the existence of a dual continuum: opposite of satisfaction is no satisfaction, and opposite of dissatisfaction is no dissatisfaction
Views of satisfaction and dissatisfaction, by the traditional view and Herzberg’s view

  • Herzberg believed that managers who sought to eliminate factors that created job dissatisfaction could bring harmony, but not motivation.

  • The extrinsic factors that create job dissatisfaction are called ==hygiene factors, ==When they are adequate, employees are neither satisfied nor dissatisfied. To motivate, he suggested using motivators, intrinsic factors such as achievement, recognition, and challenge that increase satisfaction.

  • While critics argued that the theory was too simple, it has influenced how we design jobs.

Goal-setting theory (GST)
An overview of the GST

  • Goal-setting theory says that specific goals increase performance and difficult goals when accepted result in higher performance compared to easy ones.

  • Working towards a goal is a major source of job motivation, and specific and challenging goals produce higher output than the generalised goal “do your best”

  • Employees may not try harder if they participate in the setting of goals, but participation is probably preferable to assigning goals when employees might resist accepting difficult challenges

yes, notice all the keywords in black.

  • People will do better if they get feedback on how well they’re progressing towards their goal, because feedback help identify discrepancies between what they did and will do. However, all feedback isn’t equally effective, as self-generated feedback has been shown to be a more powerful motivator than feedback from someone else

  • GST assumes that an individual is committed to a goal, and commitment is most likely when goals are public, an individual has a locus of control, and when the goal is self-set rather than assigned.

  • Self-efficacy refers to an individual’s belief that they can perform the task, and in hard situations, individuals with low self-efficacy are likely to reduce effort or give up, but individuals with high self-efficacy try harder to master.

Reinforcement Theory (RT)

  • Reinforcement theory says that behaviour is a function of its consequences. Consequences that immediately follows a behaviour and increase the probability that said behaviour will be repeated are called reinforcers.

  • It ignores things such as goals, needs, and expectations, but rather focus solely on what happens to a person when they do something.

  • According to B. F. Skinner, people will most likely engage in desired behaviours if they are rewarded for doing so, and the rewards is most effective if it immediately follows the behaviour, and behaviour that isn’t rewarded or punished is less likely to be repeated

  • Mangers can influence employee’s behaviours using positive reinforcers that help the organization reach its goals, as well as ignore and not punish the wrong behaviour, since punishment’s effects are temporary and have unwanted side effects

Equity Theory
Equity Theory

  • Developed by J. Stacey Adams, equity theory proposes that employees perceive what they get from a job situation (outcomes) in relation to what they put in (inputs)

  • If an employee perceives their ratio to be equal to relevant others, a state of equity exists. Otherwise, inequity exists and they view themselves as under-rewarded or over-rewarded

  • Not all (in)equity is real, it’s the perception that determines it. When inequity happens, employees may act on it, which results in lower or higher productivity, improved or reduced quality of output, increased absenteeism, or voluntary resignation.

  • The referent, the other people (friends, coworkers, etc), systems (pay policies, procedures, allocation), selves (input-output ratios unique to the individual), etc. that individuals compare themselves to is an important concept.

  • Historically, equity theory focused on distributive justice, the perceived fairness of the amount and allocation rewards of individuals. ==Current research ==looks into procedural justice, which is the perceived fairness of the process used to determine the distribution of rewards.

  • Research showed that distributive justice has a greater influence on employee satisfaction than procedural justice, while procedural justice tends to affect an employee’s organizational commitment, trust in his or her manager, and intention to quit.

  • Managers should openly sharing information on how allocation decisions are made, follow consistent and unbiased procedures, and engage in similar practices to increase the perception of procedural justice. Employees will be more likely to view the manager in a positive light even if they are dissatisfied with other outcomes.

Expectancy Theory
A simplified model of the Expectancy Theory

  • The most comprehensive and widely accepted theory, expectancy theory by Victor Vroom said that individuals act in certain ways based on the expectation that the act will followed by a given outcome and on the attractiveness of such outcome to the individual.

  • The explaination can be summed up in three questions:

    • How hard do I have to work to achieve a certain level of performance, and can I actually achieve that level?
    • What reward will I get for working at that level of performance?
    • How attractive is the reward to me, and does it help me achieve my goals?

  • The key to Expectancy Theory is to understand the individual’s goal and the link between effort - performance - rewards - individual goal satisfaction, and stresses that managers need to understand why employees view certain outcomes as (un)attractive.

  • The theory only concerns about perceptions, reality is irrelevant. An individual’s own perceptions of performance, reward, and goal outcomes, not the outcomes themselves will determine their motivation.

Chapter 16.1: Understanding Group Behaviour

  • A group is defined as two or more interacting and interdependent individuals who come together to achieve specific goals.

  • Formal groups are work groups defined by the organization’s structure that have designated work assignments and specific tasks

  • Informal groups are social groups which arise in the workplace due to the need for social contact, and tend to form around friendship and common interests.

Tuckman’s model of group/team development

while his name isn’t explicitly named in the textbook or slides, the citation to his research was there.
A visualization of the stages of group development

  • Groups develop through five stages:

    1. The forming stage has two phases, first when people join the group, and second when defining the group’s structure, purpose, and leadership. This stage is complete when members begin to think of themselves as part of a group
    2. The storming stage occurs as intragroup conflict happens. It is complete when there is a clear hierarchy of leadership and agreement within the group.
    3. The norming stage is one in which close relationships develop and the group becomes cohesive, and it is complete when the group developed norms regarding member behaviours.
    4. The performing stage is when the group is in structure, accepted by the members, and they started working on the group task. This is the final phase for pernament groups, but not temporary groups.
    5. The final stage for temporary groups is adjourning, where the group focuses on wrapping up activities.

Creating effective work teams
Characteristics of effective teams

  • Clear goals: A high-performing team understands their goals, have committed members, knowing what they need to accomplish, and understand how to work together to achieve such goals.

  • Relevant Skills: They are composed of individuals who have the necessary technical and interpersonal skills to work together.

  • Unified Commitment: Members express loyalty and dedication to the team and willing to do whatever it takes to succeed.

  • Good communication: Effective teams are characterized by good communication, members deliver message in ways that are easily and clearly understood. They also use feedback to guide members and correct misunderstandings.

  • Negotiation skills: Due to changing problems and relationships, members need to be able to confront and reconcile differences.

  • Appropriate Leadership: Leaders can motivate teams to follow through the the hardest challenges. by overcoming inertia, increasing team member confidence and help them recognize their goal. Studies show that when leaders show emotions at the right times, team performance is improved.

  • Internal and External Support: Internally, the team should have proper training, a clear and reasonable measurements that team members can use to evaluate their overall performance, an incentive program that recognizes and rewards team activities, and a supportive human resource system. Externally, managers should provide the team with the resources needed to get the job done.

Chapter 17: Monitoring and Controlling

The controlling process
The control process
  • The controlling process is a three-step process that includes measuring actual performance, comparing actual performance to a standard, and taking managerial actions to correct deviations or inadequate standards.

1. Measuring Performance

  • How to measure: Managers often use personal observations, statistical reports, oral reports, and written reports.

  • What to measure: a more crucial criteria compared to how to measure, if the wrong criteria is chosen it can lead to disastrous consequences. Said criteria will also be what people is most interested in excelling at.

    • Some criteria are universal, and some are dependent on the activity the manager supervise.

  • While most work activities can be expressed in quantifiable terms, when no such term is available, managers can use subjective terms instead, which is better than no term at all.

2. Comparing performance with standards

  • In all activities, it is expected to have some variation in performance, however it’s critical to determine the acceptable rate of variation. Note that performance higher than anticipated can also be a problem.
Defining the acceptable rate of variation
  • Managers can either correct the performance, or revise the standard (do nothing is also an option)

Correct Actual Performance

  • If the source of the problem is unsatisfactory work, the manager may want to take corrective action.

  • Managers who wish to correct actual performance has to choose between immediate and basic corrective action.

    • Immediate corrective action corrects problem at once to get performance back on track.
    • Basic corrective action looks at the reason of the performance derivation and and then correct its sources.

Revise the standard

  • Variance may be the result of unrealistic standards, either too low or too high. If performance constantly exceeds the goal, then the standard is too high.

  • However they need to be careful with putting standard downwards, and the problem isn’t always the standard.

3. Taking Managerial Decisions
Managerial decisions in the controlling process

Types of control
Types of control
  • All managers need appropriate tools to measure organizational performance. There are different types of control depending on the status of progression of the activity:
    - Feed-forward control happens before the activity begins.
    - Concurrent control happens as the activity is ongoing
    - Feedback control happens after the activity finishes.

Feed-forward control

  • Feed-forward control is the most anticipated type of control since it prevents anticipated problems before the activity started.

  • Problems can be prevented rather than corrected. However, they require timely and accurate information that is difficult to get.

Concurrent control

  • Concurrent control takes place as the activity progresses. As the activity happens, managers can prevent problems before they became too costly. The best known form is direct supervision.

  • Management by walking around is when a manager is out in the work area, interacting with their employees. They can monitor the actions and correct problems before it’s too late.

  • Concurrent control can be implemented by technical programs. Also, many organizational quality programs rely on it to inform their employee if they have met their standards.

Feedback control

  • Feedback control takes place after the activity is done. It is the most popular type of control.

  • Feedback control does have two advantages:

    • It provides managers with meaningful information about how effective their planning efforts were.
    • It can increase employee motivation by giving information to employees on how well they have done. However people tend to perform better when they receive positive information and vice versa.

Financial control

  • To achieve the goal of profit, managers needed financial control They may use traditional measures such as radio analysis and budget analysis.

  • There are some more popular ratios, such as:

    • Liquidity ratios that measure an organization’s ability to meet its current debt obligations.
    • Leverage ratios examine the organization’s use of debt to finance its assets and whether it’s able to meet the interest payments on the debt.
    • Activity ratios assess how efficiently a company uses its assets.
    • Profitability ratios measure how efficiently and effectively the company uses its assets to generate profits.

  • They are calculated by information from financial statements.

  • Budgets are planning and control tools. It’s a planning tool because when formulated it indicates important activities and the resources needed to be allocated to those. They are also controlling tools as they provide managers with quantitative standards of which to measure and compare resource consumption.

Information control

  • Managers deal with information controls in two ways: as a tool to help them control other organizational activities and as an organizational asset they need to protect.

How is information used in controlling?

  • Information is needed at the right time and place to measure organizational activities and performance. They also needed information of what is happening and the standards to compare to, as well as to determine if deviations are acceptable and to develop appropriate course of actions.

  • A management information system (MIS) is a system used to provide managers with needed information on a regular basis. While it can be manual or computer-based, most organizations used computer-based MISs.

    • It provide manager with information (processed and analyzed data) not merely data (unprocessed and unanalyzed facts). It collects data and turns them into relevant information for ease of use.

  • An emerging trend is the usage of dashboards that presents managers and others with a real-time, easy-to-read interface to enable decision makers to make informed decisions quickly.

Controlling information

  • Due to the importance of information within an organization, managers must have comprehensive and secure controls in place. These controls may include =data encryption, firewalls, etc==.

  • Problems can lurk in places an organization hasn’t even considered, such as blogs, websites, feeds, etc. Equipments are vulnerable to hacking.

  • Information should be monitored regularly to ensure all possible precautions are in place to protect important information.
